A Probabilistic Inference perspective of Memory Retrieval in Modern Associative Memories

HAMUX as probabilistic inference

These notes record my first attempt at understanding Associative Memory (specifically, the Hierarchical Associative Memories formed by using the rules of HAMUX) through the lens of Probabilistic Graphical Models and established techniques for probabilistic inference. Many paragraphs and examples are taken verbatim from the cited sources; I found it very helpful in places to manually transcribe the original reference notes.

With the exception of Coughlan (2009), which was used only to introduce factor graphs, I found the sources referenced in this work to be extremely relevant to our exploration. Indeed, it seems that my instinct that AM energy minimization as probabilistic inference has relevance even to exploring codes that approach the Shannon Limit. I do not yet know much about these potential applications, but I expect this to be a continually rewarding intellectual exploration that I hope unlocks future research directions for us.

Background

Factor Graphs

Overview adapted from (Coughlan 2009)

We can express many kinds of “decomposed joint probability distributions” graphically. For example, consider the Bayesian network described by the simple equation

\[ P(w,x,y,z) = P(w)P(x)P(y|w)P(z|w,x) \]

This can be expressed in the following diagram

Of course, this is not the only way the joint probability can be decomposed. Decomposing this joint probability is the structure of the problem and a choice of the researcher.

Instead of conditional probabilities, we can also model a joint distribution by decomposing it into pairwise, undirected potentials normalized by \(Z\). This form is called the Markov Random Field.

\[ P(w,x,y,z) = \frac1Z f_{wx}(w,x) f_{xz}(x,z) f_{yz}(y,z) f_{wy}(w,y). \]

This can be expressed as an undirected graph where information is passed along nodes both ways.

Expressing more complex factorizations, i.e., those where the factors are functions of more than two variables, is best accomplished by a form of hypergraph, where the edges become hyperedges, a special type of nodes themselves.

Consider the factorization \[ P(w,x,y,z) = \frac1Z f(w,x,y) g(y,z) h(z). \]

This can be drawn as the factor graph, where square nodes are the factors (hyperedges) that connect multiple nodes.

We live in the realm of undirected factor graphs (Listing 2) for the remainder of these notes, but know that there are likely strong connections to the conditional, Bayesian, directed graph of Listing 1.

Inference in General PGMs

Summarized/adapted from (Jordan 2003)

Now let us consider the general case of expressing joint probabilities as a graph. Let \(U\) denote a set of nodes of a graph, and let \(X_i\) denote the random variable associated with node \(i\), for \(i \in U\). Let \(X_C\) denote the subset of random variables associated with a subset of nodes \(C\), for any \(C \subseteq U\), and let \(X = X_U\) denote the collection of random variables associated with the graph. In the current work, we consider only the family of undirected graphs, where we describe the basic subsets as “cliques” of the graph – subsets of nodes that are completely connected. We can describe the potential of a given clique \(C\) as \(\psi_C (x_C)\), which is a general function assigning a positive real number to each configuration \(x_C\);

\[ p(x) = \frac1Z \prod_{C\in \mathcal{C}} \psi_C(x_C) \prod_{i \in U} \pi_i(x_i). \tag{1}\]

Here \(\mathcal{C}\) is the set of cliques associated with the graph, \(Z\) is the explicit normalizing factor ensuring that \(\sum_x p(x) = 1\), and \(\pi_i(x_i)\) is some prior assigned to node \(i\).

Inference

Let \((E, F)\) be a partitioning of the node indices of a graphical model into disjoint subsets, such that \((X_E, X_F)\) is a partitioning of the random variables.

We generally care about 2 kinds of inference:

  • Marginal Probabilities:

    \[ p(x_E) = \sum_{x_F} p(x_E, x_F) \]

  • Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) probabilities

    \[ p^\star(x_E) = \max_{x_F} p(x_E, x_F) \]

With these basic computations, we can compute other quantities of interest e.g., the conditional probability of latent variables \(F\) given evidence \(E\):

\[ p(x_F | x_E) = \frac{p(x_E, x_F)}{\sum_{x_F} p(x_E, x_F)}. \]

(Loopy) Belief Propagation

The goal of Belief Propagation (BP) (a.k.a., the “sum-product” algorithm) is to perform inference on our joint probability distribution. Consider the following simple joint probability:

\[ p(x) = \frac1Z \psi_{12}(x_1, x_2) \psi_{23}(x_2, x_3) \psi_{34}(x_3, x_4) \psi_{35}(x_3, x_5) \]

Say our goal is to perform inference to compute the marginal \(p(x_5)\) (also called the “belief” of \(x_5\)). We assume that an oracle can tell us the optimal elimination order in which we compute the marginals:

\[ \begin{aligned} p(x_5) &= \frac1Z \sum_{x_3} \psi_{35}(x_3, x_5) \sum_{x_4} \psi_{34}(x_3, x_4) \sum_{x_2} \psi_{23}(x_2, x_3) \sum_{x_1} \psi_{12}(x_1, x_2)\\ &= \frac1Z \sum_{x_3} \psi_{35}(x_3, x_5) \sum_{x_4} \psi_{34}(x_3, x_4) \sum_{x_2} \psi_{23}(x_2, x_3) m_{12}(x_2)\\ &= \frac1Z \sum_{x_3} \psi_{35}(x_3, x_5) \sum_{x_4} \psi_{34}(x_3, x_4) m_{23}(x_3)\\ &= \frac1Z \sum_{x_3} \psi_{35}(x_3, x_5) m_{34}(x_3)\\ &= \frac1Z m_{35}(x_5)\\ \end{aligned} \]

Note that this is a “tree-like” factor graph where the marginal can be computed exactly using a simple elimination ordering. For arbitrary graphs, it can be NP-hard to compute the best elimination ordering.

We describe the marginal

\[ m_{ij}(x_j) := \sum_{x_i} \psi_{ij}(x_i, x_j) \prod_{k \in N(i) \setminus j} m_{ki}(x_i) \tag{2}\]

to be the “message” passed from node \(i\) to node \(j\), where \(N(i)\) is the set of neighbors of node \(i\).

Note

I don’t love interpreting the notation \(m_{ij}(x_j)\) by looking at the order of indices in the subscript to represent a message from node \(i\) to node \(j\). There are cases where a message is a function of more than just the variable at the receiving node. It would be better to think of a message using the defintion of Equation 2, and know that all arguments to a message indicate a node that has not yet been summed over. For the computer scientist, we can use the convention that the first argument to the message indicates the intended “receiving node”; in the case of multiple arguments, this convention implies that our graph ordering should eliminate the first argument last.

An elegant view of the elimination process described above is that of local computation and message routing, where messages arriving at leaf nodes are necessarily marginal probabilities. However, in general we can think of a “message” as an unnormalized marginal probability

\[ p(x_i) \propto \prod \prod_{k \in N(i)} m_{ki} (x_i). \]

The above is a way that a marginal probability can be computed using BP. The same computation can be done to compute the MAP; we simply replace “sum” with “max” throughout in both cases, where the underlying justification is that “max” commutes with products just as “sum” does.

Note

The reference paper makes the following note about the similarities between message passing and neural networks:

Neural network[s] also involve message-passing algorithms and local numerical operations. An important difference, however, is that in the neural network setting each node generally has a single “activation” value that it passes to all of its neighbors. In the sum-product algorithm, on the other hand, individual messages are prepared for each neighbor. Moreover, the message \(m_{ij}(x_j)\) from \(i\) to \(j\) is not included in the product that node \(j\) forms in computing a message to send back to node \(i\). The sum-product algorithm avoids “double-counting.”

This indeed holds for traditional feed-forward networks which are not recurrent in their activations. The activations in AMs represent a form of recurrent message passing as is used in variational inference, which will be explained later.

Exact marginal computation via elimination is described excellently in (Jordan 2003), wherein the exact marginal probability for any node in an arbitrary graph can be computed using junction trees and the perfect ordering in which to eliminate the nodes.

Variational Inference in PGMs

Because exact inference is intractable for many factor graphs, we often prefer some approximation. Jordan (2003) excellently summarizes common choices for approximation e.g., Gibbs sampling and Metropolis-Hastings (forms of MCMC used often in e.g., Boltzmann machines) and importance sampling. Please refer to the reference for details on these methods.

However, Jordan (2003) describes one particular form of approximate inference in PGMs called “Variational Methods” that converts the problem of “probabilistic inference” into a problem of optimization. The difference between this method and importance sampling is that, instead of choosing a single guess distribution \(q(x)\) a priori, a family of approximating distributions \(\{q(x)\}\) is used instead. The optimization machinery chooses a particular member from this family.

Note

Liu and Wang (2019) have a modern technique for variation inference called “Stein Variational Gradient Descent”, in which they explain variational inference as:

Variational inference instead frames the Bayesian inference problem into a deterministic optimization that approximates the target distribution with a simpler distribution by minimizing their KL divergence … both the accuracy and computational cost of variational inference critically depend on the set of distributions in which the approximation is defined. Simple approximation sets, such as these used in the traditional mean field methods, are too restrictive to resemble the true posterior distributions, while more advanced choices cast more difficulties on the subsequent optimization tasks …

We begin by noting that the joint probability \(p(x)\) can be viewed as a solution to an optimization problem. In particular, define the energy of configuration \(x\) by \(E(x) = - \log p(x) - \log Z\), and define the variational free energy as

\[ \begin{aligned} F({q(x)}) &= \sum_x q(x) E(x) + \sum_x q(x) \log q(x)\\ &= -\sum_x q(x) \log p(x) + \sum_x q(x) \log q(x) - \log Z \end{aligned} \tag{3}\]

Equation 3 is equal (up to an additive constant) to the KL-divergence between \(q(x)\) and \(p(x)\), is minimized at \(q(x) = p(x)\), and attains value \(-\log Z\) at the minimum.

Minimizing an arbitrary \(F\) is as difficult as doing exact inference, and much effort has been invested into finding approximate forms of \(F\) that are easier to minimize.

Example: the Mean-Field approximation

The simplest variational algorithm is the mean field approximation, in which \(\{q(x)\}\) is restricted to the family of factorized distributions: \(q(x) = \prod_i q_i(x_i)\) (i.e., a world where all variables are independent). In this case, \(F\) from Equation 3 simplifies to

\[ F_{MF}(\{q(x)\}) = -\sum_C \sum_{x_C} \log \psi_C(x_C) \prod_{i \in C} q_i(x_i) + \sum_i \sum_{x_i} q_i (x_i) \log q_i (x_i), \]

subject to the constraint \(\sum_{x_i} q_i(x_i) = 1\). In this particular case, we can solve for the optimal distribution \(q_i^\star(x_i)\) by setting the derivative of \(F_{MF}\) w.r.t. \(q_i (x_i)\) equal to zero:

\[ q_i^\star (x_i) = \alpha \exp \left(\sum_C \sum_{x_C \setminus i} \log \psi_C (x_C) \prod_{j \in C, j \neq i} q_j (x_j) \right), \tag{4}\]

where \(\alpha\) is a normalization constant chosen so that \(\sum_{x_i} q_i (x_i) = 1\). The sum over cliques \(C\) is over all cliques that node \(i\) belongs to.

Equation 4 is an approximate inference algorithm. We initialize approximate marginals \(q_i(x_i)\) for all nodes in the graph and then update the approximate marginal at one node based on those at neighboring nodes (note that RHS of Equation 4 depends only on cliques that node \(i\) belongs to). This yields a message passing algorithm similar to neural networks; in particular, the value \(q_i(x_i)\) can be viewed as the activation of node \(i\).

Example: the Bethe approximation

The mean-field energy depends only on approximate marginals at single nodes, the Bethe free energy depends on approximate marginals at single nodes \(q_i(x_i)\) as well as approximate marginals on cliques \(q_C(x_C)\). This allows for more elaborate approximations to the free energy (Equation 3) with better approximate marginal probabilities:

\[ F_\beta (\{q_i, q_C\}) = \sum_C \sum_{x_C} q_C(x_C) \log \frac{q_C (x_C)}{\psi_C(x_C)} - \sum_i (d_i - 1) \sum_{x_i} q_i (x_i) \log q_i (x_i), \tag{5}\]

where \(d_i - 1\) is the number of cliques that node \(i\) belongs to.

Note

The approximate clique marginals and the approximate singleton marginals must satisfy a simple marginalization constraint: \(\sum_{x_C \setminus i} q_C(x_C) = q_i (x_i)\). When we add Lagrange multipliers and differentiate the Lagrangian we obtain a set of fixed point equations. Surprisingly, these equations end up being equivalent to the “sum-product” algorithm for trees (Equation 2). The messages $m_{ij}(x_j) are simply exponentiated Lagrange multipliers. Thus, the Bethe approximation is equivalent to applying the local message passing scheme developed for trees to graphs that have loops (see Yedidia, Freeman, and Weiss (2001)). This approach to approximate inference algorithms has been very successful in the domain of error-control coding, allowing practical codes based on graphical models to nearly reach the Shannon Limit.

From PGMs to HAMUX

Turning probabilities into energy

Converting a joint probability (Equation 1) to a joint energy is possible through the Boltzmann distribution:

\[\begin{align} p(x) &= \frac{ \exp (- E(x))}{Z} \\ \implies E(x) &= - \log p(x) - \log Z \\ &= - \sum_{C \in \mathcal{C}} \log \psi_C(x_C) - \sum_{i \in U} \log \tilde{\pi}_i(x_i) - \log Z \end{align}\]

We will drop the constant shift \(\log Z\) and use the energy potential \(\phi_C(x_C) := -\log \psi_C(x_C)\) to denote the new “energy factors” and \(\tilde{\pi}_i(x_i) := -\log \pi_i(x_i)\) to denote the “energy priors”. Thus,

\[ E(x) = \sum_{C \in \mathcal{C}} \phi_C(x_C) + \sum_{i} \tilde{\pi}_i(x_i), \tag{6}\]

which is the general form of the energy hypergraph used in HAMUX. We call the energy factor \(\phi_C(x_C)\) an interaction or synaptic energy and \(\tilde{\pi}_i(x_i)\) the neuron energy.

Energy Minimization of AMs is variational inference in PGMs
